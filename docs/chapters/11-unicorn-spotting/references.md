# References: Unicorn Spotting — Separating Fact from Fantasy in Tech Claims

1. [Media literacy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Media_literacy) - Wikipedia - Framework for critically evaluating information sources and claims, the foundational skill this chapter applies to technology press releases and investor pitches.

2. [Hype cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hype_cycle) - Wikipedia - Gartner's model for tracking technology maturity and adoption, providing the classification system this chapter teaches students to apply to real-world claims.

3. [Confirmation bias](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confirmation_bias) - Wikipedia - Psychological research on how people selectively interpret information to support existing beliefs, explaining why unicorn claims persist despite contrary evidence.

4. Calling Bullshit: The Art of Skepticism in a Data-Driven World - Carl T. Bergstrom and Jevin D. West - Random House - University of Washington professors' guide to identifying misleading claims, the intellectual ancestor of this chapter's unicorn-spotting methodology.

5. AI Snake Oil: What Artificial Intelligence Can Do, What It Can't, and How to Tell the Difference - Arvind Narayanan and Sayash Kapoor - Princeton University Press - Princeton researchers' systematic debunking of exaggerated AI claims, the closest real-world equivalent to this chapter's field guide.

6. [AI Incident Database](https://incidentdatabase.ai/) - Partnership on AI - Catalog of real-world AI failures and harms, providing documented evidence for evaluating claims against actual performance.

7. [Snopes Fact-Checking Methodology](https://www.snopes.com/about-snopes/) - Snopes - Overview of professional fact-checking processes that students can adapt for evaluating technology claims using the chapter's framework.

8. [Critical Thinking and AI Claims](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/how-to-evaluate-ai-claims/) - Brookings Institution - Policy researchers' guide to assessing AI capabilities versus marketing, a sober companion to the chapter's satirical approach.

9. [The Turk: The Life and Times of the Famous Automaton](https://publicdomainreview.org/collection/the-turk/) - Public Domain Review - History of the 18th-century chess-playing automaton hoax, proving that fake AI demonstrations predate actual AI by two centuries.

10. [CRAAP Test for Evaluating Sources](https://library.csuchico.edu/help/source-or-information-good) - California State University, Chico - Library science framework for evaluating information credibility that maps directly to the chapter's unicorn verification checklist.
